AI Boom and Housing in San Francisco: A Data-Driven Analysis

By Hammon Dutra, Marcel Pereira, Van Anh Nguyen, Tiago dos Santos, Yua Tsuchihashi
March 19, 2025 8 min read

Introduction

San Francisco’s dual identity as a global AI hub and a city grappling with housing unaffordability presents a critical paradox. Between 2017 and 2024, the AI sector’s expansion—42% of U.S. AI firms are based in the Bay Area—has coincided with volatile rent trends, and assumptions about direct causation (Global AI Market Report, 2023). This article analyzes the interplay between the AI-driven tech boom and housing costs, comparing pre-pandemic (2017–2019) and post-pandemic (2022–2024) periods, with the question of How historical housing data is impacted by the tech boom (e.g. rental prices, property values, availability). Key advancements, such as the 2017 transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), catalyzed corporate growth, yet inflation-adjusted rents in AI-heavy neighborhoods like Potrero Hill rose only moderately, while non-tech areas like zip codes 94111 and 94112 saw sharper increases (San Francisco Housing Authority, 2023). Historical precedents further complicate this relationship: the 2014–2015 tech boom spiked rents citywide, but subsequent layoffs and the COVID-19 pandemic (2019–2023) temporarily stabilized prices (Zuk et al., 2018; Urban Displacement Project, 2021). Using mixed-methods analysis—rent data, corporate leases, and employee interviews—this study reveals spatially uneven impacts, urging a reevaluation of how technological growth intersects with urban equity.

Rent Prices in San Francisco

We have figured out the rent prices from 2023 to 2025, divided by regions. There is no significant change in prices in these two years, and prices fluctuate, but not too much. Figuring out the rate of change, there was a price rise in only two areas: Zipcode 94112 and 94111. This zip code covers Financial District (94111) and Mission Terrace (94112) in San Francisco. Financial District (94111) is known for the city’s central business hubs, with notable firms including Wells Fargo, Visa, Salesforce, and PG&E. However, this area is not the area in which AI and tech companies are concentrated. Mission Terrace (94112) is known for its residential area and fewer commercial offices. It is unlikely to host AI offices in these areas. These results may indicate that the causation between the significant increase in the number of offices in the SF Bay area and rent prices in SF is weak.

Conclusion

Rent Prices in San Francisco

San Francisco’s housing crisis reflects a nuanced relationship with its AI boom, shaped by geography, policy, and external shocks. While 42% of U.S. AI firms cluster in the Bay Area, rent increases post-2017 were concentrated in neighborhoods like Potrero Hill, not AI office hubs. Citywide, only two non-tech zip codes (94111, 94112) saw significant hikes (San Francisco Housing Authority, 2023), underscoring macroeconomic drivers—speculative investment, housing shortages—over direct industry impact. The pandemic’s rent dip and post-2022 rebound further highlight systemic forces, including return-to-office mandates, outweighing AI’s localized influence.

Neighborhood-specific trends, such as the Mission District’s alignment with tech layoffs (2016–2017) and recovery post-transformers adoption, reveal how zoning and economic cycles mediate housing outcomes. Moving forward, equitable solutions require expanding affordable housing, corporate accountability (e.g., remote-work policies), and leveraging AI innovations for predictive urban planning. By addressing systemic inequities rather than scapegoating tech growth, San Francisco can model resilient urban development in the AI era.
